# Behavioral Assessment - Example Scenarios

This document shows real examples of the behavioral scenarios students will encounter, organized by job role.

---

## Frontend Developer Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Client Scope Creep
**Situation**: A client requests major design changes after you've completed 80% of development. The changes require reworking core functionality.

**Question**: How do you handle this situation?

**Options**:
- A) Explain the impact clearly, provide timeline & cost estimates, offer alternatives ✓ (95/100)
- B) Refuse the change as it's too late to modify (30/100)
- C) Make the changes without discussing timeline/impact (50/100)
- D) Ask the client to choose between original and new design (75/100)

**Skills Tested**: Communication, Problem-solving, Client Management

---

### Scenario 2: Code Review Rejection
**Situation**: Your code review feedback is rejected by a senior colleague who says "it works fine, stop overthinking."

**Question**: What's your next step?

**Options**:
- A) Let it go - they have more experience (40/100)
- B) Escalate immediately to management (45/100)
- C) Explain specific risks with concrete examples, suggest pairing to review together ✓ (90/100)
- D) Document concerns and let the issue become a bug later (25/100)

**Skills Tested**: Communication, Professionalism, Technical Judgment

---

### Scenario 3: Security Vulnerability
**Situation**: You discover a security vulnerability in production that could expose user data. The manager is in a meeting.

**Question**: What do you do?

**Options**:
- A) Fix it immediately without notifying anyone (50/100)
- B) Wait for the manager to finish their meeting (20/100)
- C) Interrupt the meeting, explain severity, coordinate immediate fix with team ✓ (100/100)
- D) Send an email to the manager (60/100)

**Skills Tested**: Judgment, Responsibility, Communication

---

### Scenario 4: Mentoring Struggling Team Member
**Situation**: A new team member makes frequent mistakes in code. They're becoming defensive about feedback.

**Question**: How do you help them improve?

**Options**:
- A) Request they be moved to a different team (15/100)
- B) Focus on positive feedback, offer to pair program, understand their struggles ✓ (95/100)
- C) Continue giving direct critical feedback (35/100)
- D) Document all their mistakes for performance review (25/100)

**Skills Tested**: Empathy, Mentoring, Communication

---

### Scenario 5: Missed Deadline
**Situation**: You realize you missed a deadline because you underestimated task complexity.

**Question**: What's your approach?

**Options**:
- A) Blame external factors or others (10/100)
- B) Own the mistake, explain what happened, provide new timeline, prevent future ✓ (100/100)
- C) Hope no one notices the delay (5/100)
- D) Reduce quality to meet deadline (40/100)

**Skills Tested**: Accountability, Communication, Professionalism

## Data Analyst Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Analysis Contradicts Leadership
**Situation**: Your analysis shows a product the company is about to launch has serious usability issues. Your manager disagrees with your findings.

**Question**: How do you proceed?

**Options**:
- A) Withdraw your analysis to avoid conflict (20/100)
- B) Present additional data, explain methodology, offer to validate with users ✓ (95/100)
- C) Go public with concerns on social media (15/100)
- D) Keep quiet and let the product launch (10/100)

**Skills Tested**: Integrity, Communication, Conviction

---

### Scenario 2: Data Entry Error
**Situation**: You find a data entry error that affects 3 months of historical reporting.

**Question**: What do you do?

**Options**:
- A) Quietly correct it without telling anyone (30/100)
- B) Document the error, notify stakeholders, provide corrected analysis ✓ (100/100)
- C) Assume it's not significant enough to report (15/100)
- D) Report it weeks later after impact is clear (40/100)

**Skills Tested**: Integrity, Responsibility, Communication

---

### Scenario 3: Pressured Data Manipulation
**Situation**: A colleague asks you to help show better numbers by adjusting data interpretation (not falsifying).

**Question**: Your response?

**Options**:
- A) Help them - it's just interpretation (15/100)
- B) Decline clearly, explain why data integrity matters, suggest honest alternatives ✓ (100/100)
- C) Escalate without talking to them first (55/100)
- D) Go along but feel uncomfortable (25/100)

**Skills Tested**: Integrity, Judgment, Communication

---

### Scenario 4: Uncertain Data Quality
**Situation**: Your dashboard shows a metric declining. The team says the data collection method might be wrong.

**Question**: How do you validate?

**Options**:
- A) Trust the team and ignore the metric (30/100)
- B) Audit the collection method, cross-reference with other sources, clarify status ✓ (100/100)
- C) Report it as-is without validation (40/100)
- D) Assume the data is correct (35/100)

**Skills Tested**: Rigor, Skepticism, Validation

---

### Scenario 5: Urgent Analysis with Limited Time
**Situation**: You're asked to provide quick analysis for an urgent decision, but full analysis needs 3 days.

**Question**: What do you suggest?

**Options**:
- A) Provide incomplete analysis to save time (25/100)
- B) Offer preliminary insights with clear limitations, full analysis later ✓ (95/100)
- C) Tell them to wait 3 days (50/100)
- D) Make assumptions to fill gaps (30/100)

**Skills Tested**: Pragmatism, Communication, Judgment

## HR Manager Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Two Excellent Candidates
**Situation**: Two excellent candidates for a critical role: one has perfect technical skills but poor communication; one has good skills but exceptional people skills.

**Question**: Who do you hire and why?

**Options**:
- A) Hire the technical expert - skills are most important (40/100)
- B) Hire the people person - soft skills matter more (45/100)
- C) Assess role requirements, offer training for weak area, hire best overall fit ✓ (95/100)
- D) Reject both and keep searching (60/100)

**Skills Tested**: Judgment, Strategic Thinking, Decision-making

---

### Scenario 2: Serious Misconduct Report
**Situation**: An employee reports their manager for inappropriate behavior. The manager is a top performer and friend of leadership.

**Question**: What's your approach?

**Options**:
- A) Investigate thoroughly, prioritize reporter safety, enforce policy equally ✓ (100/100)
- B) Downplay the issue to protect manager's reputation (10/100)
- C) Suggest the employee leave to avoid conflict (20/100)
- D) Handle it informally between the parties (30/100)

**Skills Tested**: Integrity, Fairness, Responsibility

---

### Scenario 3: Workforce Reduction
**Situation**: You need to reduce staff by 15%. Which employees should be let go?

**Question**: How do you make this decision?

**Options**:
- A) Based purely on seniority - newest employees first (40/100)
- B) Evaluate performance, skills, fit for future, treat with dignity, provide support ✓ (95/100)
- C) Choose randomly to be fair (15/100)
- D) Let managers choose their lowest performers (50/100)

**Skills Tested**: Strategic Thinking, Fairness, Compassion

---

### Scenario 4: Medical Accommodation
**Situation**: An employee requests accommodation for a medical condition. It requires significant operational change.

**Question**: How do you proceed?

**Options**:
- A) Deny it - operational burden is too high (15/100)

### Scenario 5: Pay Inequity Discovery
**Situation**: You discover pay inequity between employees doing the same role. Budget is tight.

**Question**: What's your action plan?

- A) Ignore it - can't afford to fix now (15/100)

## Product Manager Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Research Contradicts Plans
**Situation**: User research shows your planned feature contradicts actual user needs. You've already budgeted development time.

**Question**: What do you do?

**Options**:
- A) Proceed anyway - sunk cost is already invested (15/100)
- B) Pivot based on research, reallocate budget, validate new direction with users ✓ (100/100)
- C) Ignore research and build what you planned (20/100)
- D) Build both features to hedge bets (55/100)

**Skills Tested**: Customer Focus, Decision-making, Judgment

---

### Scenario 2: Engineering vs Customer Demand
**Situation**: Engineering says a feature is technically impossible. But customers are demanding it.

**Question**: How do you handle this?

**Options**:
- A) Promise customers anyway - figure it out later (20/100)
- B) Accept engineering verdict and disappoint customers (50/100)
- C) Deep dive with engineering, explore alternatives, communicate honestly with customers ✓ (95/100)
- D) Go around engineering and find another team (35/100)

**Skills Tested**: Problem-solving, Communication, Cross-functional

---

### Scenario 3: Risk vs Safety Decision
**Situation**: Two product visions: one safe (incremental improvements) and one risky (transformational). Leadership is split.

**Question**: How do you decide?

**Options**:
- A) Always choose safe - minimize risk (40/100)
- B) Always choose risky - big bets win (45/100)
- C) Analyze data, market timing, resources, competitive position, decide with clarity ✓ (95/100)
- D) Let leadership decide - defer accountability (30/100)

**Skills Tested**: Strategic Thinking, Decision-making, Risk Assessment

---

### Scenario 4: Bug vs Feature Trade-off
**Situation**: A critical bug affects 2% of users. Fixing it requires delaying a major feature launch.

**Question**: What's your call?

**Options**:
- A) Launch anyway - 2% is acceptable (25/100)
- B) Delay launch - fix the bug first (70/100)
- C) Assess impact severity, user tier affected, mitigation options, then decide ✓ (95/100)
- D) Launch with known bug - customers will report if serious (20/100)

**Skills Tested**: Judgment, Risk Assessment, Stakeholder Management

---

### Scenario 5: Wrong Assumptions
**Situation**: You realize your product roadmap is based on assumptions that may be wrong.

**Question**: How do you respond?

**Options**:
- A) Continue as planned - can't change now (20/100)
- B) Validate assumptions immediately, adjust roadmap, communicate changes transparently ✓ (100/100)
- C) Hope they turn out to be correct (10/100)
- D) Blame others for the bad assumptions (15/100)

**Skills Tested**: Intellectual Honesty, Accountability, Adaptability

## Scoring Patterns

### Excellent Responses (90-100)
- Take responsibility
- Communicate clearly
- Seek information/validation
- Consider multiple perspectives
- Look for win-win solutions
- Maintain integrity
- Prioritize people and process

### Good Responses (70-85)
- Generally sound approach
- Some communication
- Reasonable judgment
- Minor gaps in execution
- Mostly ethical

### Average Responses (50-70)
- Partially addressed
- Some confusion
- Limited communication
- Could be better

### Weak Responses (0-45)
- Avoids responsibility
- Poor communication
- Unethical choices
- Ignores impact on others
- Rushed decisions
